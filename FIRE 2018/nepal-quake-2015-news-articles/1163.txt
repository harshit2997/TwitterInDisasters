Right after I moved to Kathmandu in February 2006, I was told about the inevitable Big One. This majestic and troubled country sat on one of the most dangerous fault lines in the world, and Kathmandu was built on the dried bed of an ancient lake. The ground beneath the city is soft and the power of an earthquake would be amplified through it. Every 70 years, many people warned me, a major quake hits, and already 72 years had passed since the last one.

At the time, Nepal was at war. A Maoist insurgency had gained control of much of the countryside, particularly in the hills where most people live a great distance from any drivable road and state services. King Gyanendra had abolished parliament and ruled the country as a dictator. The sense of fear, even in Kathmandu, was palpable; human rights activists hid in safe houses, and local government officials faced constant threats and assassinations for doing their jobs. Schools became recruitment grounds for child soldiers. Thousands of people disappeared.
